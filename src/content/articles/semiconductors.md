Semiconductors lie at the heart of modern technology. From smartphones and computers to solar panels and satellites, nearly every modern electronic device relies on semiconductor materials. Yet the process used to manufacture these crystals on Earth is fundamentally limited by physics — particularly by convection driven by gravity.

Although engineers have developed techniques to mitigate these effects, a more radical idea has emerged: **why not manufacture semiconductors in space?**

---

## A Factory in Orbit

In 2025, the company **Space Forge** took a step toward this goal by launching **ForgeStar-1**, a satellite carrying a miniature remotely operable furnace. The system was activated in December, reaching temperatures of nearly **1000 °C** and generating plasma.

The mission served as a proof-of-concept experiment demonstrating that semiconductor manufacturing processes can operate in orbit.

---

## Why Leave the Planet?

The primary motivation for producing semiconductors in orbit is the presence of **microgravity**. On Earth, buoyancy causes hot fluid to rise while colder fluid sinks. These motions create convection currents within molten semiconductor material.

These currents transport impurities and dopants unevenly through the melt, producing crystals with non-uniform properties.

## Microgravity and Its Advantages

In orbit, the effective value of **g** in the Rayleigh number becomes extremely small. As a result, buoyancy-driven convection is greatly suppressed.

Crystal growth then occurs primarily through diffusion, producing significantly more uniform materials.

Space Forge claims that this environment could allow semiconductors to be produced that are up to **4000 times purer** than those grown on Earth.

---

## Remaining Challenges

Despite the advantages of microgravity, crystal growth in space is not completely free from fluid motion. One important mechanism is **surface-tension-driven convection**, also known as the **Marangoni effect**.

Another major obstacle is economic rather than physical: launching and retrieving material from orbit remains extremely expensive.

---

## The Next Steps

While ForgeStar-1 was primarily a demonstration mission and will not return to Earth, the follow-up mission **ForgeStar-2** aims to demonstrate the safe recovery of materials manufactured in orbit.

If successful, this technology could eventually lead to **orbital manufacturing facilities**, where reusable satellites act as miniature factories producing ultra-high-purity semiconductor materials.
